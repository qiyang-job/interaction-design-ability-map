## Design Context

### Users
This tool is for junior to mid-level interaction designers and product designers who do many small UX improvements in real projects, then need to review, explain, and accumulate those improvements as design capability evidence.

They use it while studying interaction design, preparing project reviews, writing weekly summaries, building portfolios, or clarifying whether a problem belongs to flow, information architecture, layout, operation, status feedback, or rule logic.

### Brand Personality
Calm, precise, practical.

The interface should feel like a focused design study workspace: clear, restrained, and trustworthy. It should help users feel that messy design work can be organized into a repeatable judgment system.

### Aesthetic Direction
Use a black, white, and gray neutral system with restrained line icons. The right references are professional product-design documentation, Linear-like work surfaces, Apple HIG-like clarity, and Notion-like scanability. The wrong references are marketing landing pages, oversized cards, decorative gradients, thick accent borders, heavy table UIs, and AI-generated card stacks.

### Design Principles
- Start with aggregation, then allow detail. Every section should first answer "what should I remember" before listing supporting explanation.
- Keep reading pressure low. Prefer short labels, grouped evidence, compact summaries, and progressive comparison over long descriptive paragraphs.
- Use structure instead of decoration. Rely on alignment, spacing, light dividers, and typography before adding surfaces or borders.
- Avoid hard tables unless comparison truly needs a grid. When table-like content appears, make it feel like a worksheet or method panel, not a spreadsheet pasted onto a page.
- Make the product itself demonstrate good interaction design: clear hierarchy, predictable navigation, responsive behavior, and focused use of icons.
